Macro Overlay · Iran / Hormuz · BofA / JPM Slide 11 / 18

The right question is not whether AI demand survives — it does. The question is whether physical deployment stays on schedule and at acceptable returns.

How Macro Transmits into Tech (BofA)
  • Physical disruptions at Hormuz → shipping cost and insurance premia rise
  • CPI at 2.5%; core PCE near 40bp — Fed stays on hold; no capex financing cost relief
  • Higher energy = higher data-center opex (cooling power, generation fuel)
  • Grid constraints worsen as energy infrastructure prioritization shifts
  • JPM: SPR releases are "almost useless" as a logistics fix — six policy options, none solve the routing / insurance confidence problem immediately
What Changes vs What Doesn't
  • Unchanged Underlying AI demand is secular and not oil-price sensitive
  • Unchanged Oracle RPO of $553B is contracted backlog — not revocable on oil price
  • Unchanged Hyperscaler capex budgets largely committed through 2026
  • Changes Build timing may slip if fuel, logistics, or power procurement delays project starts
  • Changes Construction cost inflation raised by energy, materials, shipping inputs
  • Changes Return-on-capital calculations shift as power opex becomes more expensive
Bull case: Brent normalizes to $65–70

Morgan Stanley de-escalation path. Oil retraces; AI build pace is unaffected; deployment costs normalize. AI infra beneficiaries rerate across the full stack.

Bear case: Brent sustained at $120–130

Prolonged Hormuz closure. Fuel and logistics constrain build pace. Power scarcity worsens. Capital-intensive builds see schedule slippage and return compression.

Right analytical frame

Deployment bottlenecks, not demand destruction. Build timing and infrastructure availability are now key return variables the market has historically underweighted in tech capex models.

Institutional Takeaways · Positioning Slide 18 / 18

The densification thesis — not the chip thesis — is the highest-conviction call in this dataset.

The Widening Stack
Strategic advantage now depends on controlling or partnering across compute, optical interconnect, packaging, power conversion, thermal management, site power / generation, and cloud capacity financing.
The largest near-term risk is not a collapse in AI demand. It is a mismatch between demand and the physical ability to deploy capacity on time and at acceptable economics. That is a new variable the market has historically underweighted in AI capex models. Power procurement delays, energy cost inflation, and over-crowding in winning names are the three most actionable near-term risk factors — not the secular demand outlook.
