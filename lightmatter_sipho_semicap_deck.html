Architecture Analysis

Rubin Is a Hybrid Electro-Optical Package — Optics at the Edge, Copper at the Core

Understanding the architecture is prerequisite to scoping which equipment categories experience incremental demand

  • Not a full interposer replacement. Lightmatter's SiPho tile is better understood as CoWoS-L-like — a localized silicon-plus-optics die added to the package, not a base interposer replacing the entire electrical fabric.
  • Primary role: package-edge I/O bandwidth. SiPho relieves the off-package bottleneck where optical long-reach connectivity has a clear advantage over copper.
  • Core GPU-HBM fabric stays copper. The dense, short-reach GPU-to-HBM interface remains electrical — still the optimal solution for local high-bandwidth memory at close proximity.
  • Model assumption: 1 SiPho interposer per Rubin package. Interposer demand therefore scales 1:1 with Rubin volume, adjusted only for assembly yield (95%).

Category Deep Dive · Inspection & Metrology

Photonics Is Exquisitely Sensitive to Process Variation — Inspection Becomes a Yield Lifeline

26 combined tools / $91M — secondary in dollars but essential to achieving the 50% FE yield assumption; worse without it

  • Photonics yield is highly CD-sensitive. Waveguide width tolerances are sub-nm; any CD deviation shifts the optical transmission window and kills performance even on structurally intact die.
  • 5 insertions per wafer assumed. Both optical inspection and metrology tools are modeled at 5 passes/wafer — cover overlay, film thickness, waveguide CD, and surface defects at multiple process stages.
  • Combined tool count: 13 each, 26 total. Onto (optical surface inspection) and KLA (wafer-level metrology) are the reference platforms, modeled at $3.5M and ~$3M ASPs respectively.
  • Economically secondary, strategically essential. At $91M combined, inspection is a rounding error vs. litho — but without it, yield degrades and the wafer start requirement (and litho demand) climbs sharply.

Risk Analysis

Three Structural Risks Could Materially Compress the Equipment Pool

Bull case is $2.26B; bear case compresses substantially depending on yield trajectory, throughput realization, and cycle dynamics

Risk 1: FE Yield Improvement Reduces Wafer Starts (and Equipment Demand)

Base case assumes 50% FE yield — a conservative SiPho penalty assumption. If yield improves to 65–80% through process learning, annual wafer starts drop 23–38%, compressing litho tool demand by 5–9 scanners and shrinking the equipment pool by ~$400–700M. This is an upside risk for Lightmatter but a downside for ASML and EO test suppliers.

Risk 2: Fiber Attach Throughput Stalls and Gates Volume Ramp

Active alignment of 256 fibers/package at scale has no established industrial playbook. Model assumes 12 UPH; if throughput stalls at 6–8 UPH, line count roughly doubles but the physical ramp may not be achievable, effectively capping Rubin volume below 5M packages/yr regardless of litho and test capacity.

Risk 3: 2028 Cycle Cliff Creates Stranded Capex

Rubin volume drops 80% in 2028 (1M units) versus 2026–27 peak (5M). Tools ordered for peak capacity — particularly EO test systems and fiber attach lines with limited secondary-market demand — face significant utilization headwinds in the trough year.

Key Takeaways

Four Analytical Conclusions From the SiPho Equipment Model

Structured around architecture logic, equipment sizing, risk calibration, and strategic positioning

1
Architecture Scope Is Decisive for Equipment Selection
Because SiPho is a package-edge tile rather than a full interposer, only 5 equipment categories see step-change incremental demand. Confusing this with a full-interposer replacement would misidentify the tool set entirely.
2
Litho Revenue Concentration Is a Direct Consequence of Die Size
The 83% scanner throughput haircut from stitching is not a model assumption — it is a geometric consequence of a >4,000 mm² die on a 26×33mm reticle field. 78% of the equipment pool flowing to ASML is structurally locked in.
3
EO Test Is the Model's Most Analytically Interesting Category
119 systems with zero CoWoS precedent, forming a genuinely incremental spend pool. 70-min/wafer test time creates a fleet that rivals inspection in unit count despite a lower ASP — and is the clearest structural proof that SiPho changes the equipment map.
4
Fiber Attach Throughput Is the Volume Ramp's Weakest Link
At 12 UPH for 256-fiber active alignment, fiber attach is the hardest step to industrialize and the most uncertain input in the model. Achieving 5M packages/yr may require either a step-change in throughput or a rethink of the fiber coupling architecture.
